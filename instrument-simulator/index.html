About the Piri

What is it?

The piri (피리) is a Korean double-reed bamboo oboe, producing a distinctive nasal and penetrating tone. It consists of a cylindrical bamboo body with eight finger holes (seven on top, one on bottom) and a large double reed made from reed grass.

History

The piri originated from Central Asian instruments and was introduced to Korea during the Three Kingdoms period (57 BCE - 668 CE). It became integral to Korean court music (jeongak) and folk music traditions. Historical records from the Goryeo Dynasty (918-1392) document its prominent role in royal ceremonies.

Cultural Significance

The piri holds deep cultural meaning in Korea as the voice of emotion in traditional ensembles. Its haunting tone is said to express han (한) - a uniquely Korean concept of deep sorrow and longing. It leads melodic lines in court orchestras and accompanies shamanic rituals, folk songs, and traditional dance. The instrument is classified as an Important Intangible Cultural Property of Korea.

About the Haegeum

What is it?

The haegeum (해금) is a two-stringed vertical fiddle with a rodlike neck and hollow wooden soundbox. It uses a rosined bow made of forsythia wood and horsehair, played by drawing the bow between its two silk strings. The instrument has no fingerboard - the player stops the strings by pressing with their fingernails.

History

The haegeum descended from the Chinese erhu and Central Asian fiddles, entering Korea during the Goryeo Dynasty (918-1392). Originally used in military processions, it evolved to become essential in both court and folk music. The Akhak Gwebeom (1493), a Korean musical treatise, provides detailed documentation of the instrument's construction and playing techniques.

Cultural Significance

The haegeum is often called the instrument that "speaks" in Korean music due to its vocal-like quality. It can express the full range of human emotions, from profound sorrow to joyful celebration. In pansori (epic storytelling through song), it provides crucial accompaniment. The haegeum bridges court and folk traditions, appearing in royal ancestral rites and village festivals alike. It represents the Korean aesthetic of "sound that breathes."

About the Janggu

What is it?

The janggu (장구), also called janggo, is an hourglass-shaped double-headed drum. Its distinctive shape creates two different sound chambers: the left head (궁편) is covered with thick cowhide for deep bass tones, while the right head (채편) uses thinner horsehide or dogskin for sharp, high-pitched sounds. Players use a round wooden mallet on the left and a thin bamboo stick on the right.

History

The janggu arrived in Korea from China during the Three Kingdoms period, with earliest records dating to the 7th century. It became the rhythmic backbone of Korean music by the Goryeo Dynasty. The instrument's name comes from the Chinese "zhanggu" (杖鼓). Over centuries, it evolved from a court instrument to an essential part of folk music, farmers' music (nongak), and shamanic ceremonies.

Cultural Significance

The janggu is considered the heartbeat of Korean traditional music, controlling tempo and providing the rhythmic foundation for all ensemble performances. Its two contrasting voices symbolize the harmony of opposites in Korean philosophy - yin and yang, earth and heaven. It is indispensable in samulnori (percussion ensemble), pungmul (farmers' dance), and court music. Learning janggu rhythm patterns (jangdan) is fundamental to understanding Korean musical structure. The instrument represents Korea's vibrant rhythmic heritage and communal spirit.
